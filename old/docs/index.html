With my lady and I in St. Augustine for our anniversary, we thought visiting the Medieval Torture Museum would be cool to see. We expected a few visual examples, maybe some relics of the past detailing the history of torture. From the outside, we didn't anticipate the museum would suck you in as deeply and with such graphic replicas of torture devices and human suffering. But holy shit, the depictions, combined with the headphones or your phone to do the audio tour, gave you as good a glimpse without experiencing it first hand of all the horrific things human beings have done to each other. Hell, I discovered some devices and approaches I hadn't even heard of before. If you're squeamish, chances are you will not want to pay a visit. But if you're into the macabre and the side of human history, I don't think you will have a disappointing experience.

Upon paying your ticket, you walk through the entry point in a darkened, medieval-like atmosphere that becomes darker and more gruesome with their exhibits as you go further down the hallway into more of the rooms. Again, having the audio explaining the devices and methods of torture enhances the experience as you tour the exhibits.

To give you an idea, you view devices such as the garotte and gallows used for hanging, a guillotine, a rack for using to pull apart people's limbs. As we made our way down the hall, we saw the various masks used for blaspheming, chastity devices, followed by a brazen bull -- which, if you know anything about Roman history, was some pretty intense shit. The breaking on the wheel... also some severe epic shit. That was all fine and dandy, dandy and fine. However, the exhibits become so much more graphic and gruesome than I anticipated.

Such as the scaffolding used to flay the skin off of people's bodies. Yeah, they showed a replica of that. Things started to become real from there. The previous devices, I had read about in history books and watched documentaries. No big deal. But flaying is another level. How they used the cat-o-nine tails and cat's claw, also was on another level. Seeing the examples, using prosthetic models, of how these torture devices worked enhanced the visual experience of seeing how this was used in medieval times and earlier.

As you moved deeper down the hall, the methods of torture become crueler and more barbaric, as if that wasn't possible already if you didn't know how far people went to torture. The heretics fork and seeing how torturers used that, with the example on a prosthetic model, demonstrated some crazy shit; devices to crush hands and fingers, pulling and cutting out tongues, impaling, an iron maiden, a pendulum -- also insane shit.

By the time you finished taking the tour, if you thought you knew about torture before, you certainly did now. Not for the weak of hearted for sure. This tour is definitely for people that have an interest in medieval history, especially torture. This tour is not Disney on Torture. It's a great exhibit and will leave you with visuals.

I just may not use an anniversary or birthday as an occasion to do the tour, unless you're dark and macabre like that. But hey, to each their own.

The gallery includes 25-30 tiny paintings, etchings, and sculptures viewed through microscopes. Most are literally in the eyes of needles. The details are amazing! I particularly liked the sailing ship. There is one exhibit that is invisible to the naked eye and barely visible with the microscope.

I highly recommend a visit to the Micro Masterpieces Art Gallery.
